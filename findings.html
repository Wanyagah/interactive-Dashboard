What the Data Tells Us
About Women in Education

An analysis of 11,901 country-year observations spanning 170 nations from 1998 to 2019, drawn from the World Bank Gender Statistics indicator SE.TER.GRAD.FE.ZS. The findings reveal a consistent and widening structural split — women dominate certain fields and remain significantly underrepresented in others, with slow but measurable progress everywhere.

Finding 01

A Persistent Structural Split Across Fields

The single most striking finding in this dataset is not a trend over time — it is the magnitude and consistency of field-level segregation. Female graduates are strongly majority in four fields and strongly minority in two, with little variation across countries or years.

Education (72.6%) and Health & Welfare (71.5%) sit more than 22 percentage points above gender parity. Engineering (26.7%) sits 23 points below it. This 46-point gap between the most and least female-dominated fields has been stable across two decades of data.

The 50% parity line is a useful anchor. Fields above it — Education, Health, Arts & Humanities, Social Sciences, Natural Sciences — account for a large share of tertiary enrolment. Fields below parity — Services, Agriculture, STEM (aggregate), Engineering — are fewer but economically consequential.

Notably, Natural Sciences, Maths & Statistics (55%) has crossed parity, while the STEM aggregate (32%) remains well below it — a gap explained by the Engineering component dragging the composite down.

Finding 02

Slow but Universal Progress Since 1998

Every field except "Unknown or unspecified" showed a positive trend over the observation period. The gains are modest in absolute terms — most fields improved by 3–8 percentage points over ~20 years — but the consistency across fields is noteworthy.

Natural Sciences, Maths & Statistics posted the largest gain: +8.1 pp, crossing the parity threshold in the process. STEM (aggregate) improved by +6.9 pp, and Agriculture by +6.2 pp — suggesting that fields which were below parity are catching up faster than fields already above it.

Finding 03

Engineering Remains the Hardest Barrier

Engineering, Manufacturing & Construction is the most persistently male-dominated field in the dataset, with a global average of just 26.7% female graduates. This is not driven by a few outliers — only 5 countries have ever exceeded 50% female engineering graduates in any year with data.

Argentina leads all countries with a remarkable 75.3% female share in Engineering (2011), a figure that is a substantial outlier globally. Beyond that, only Benin (54.6%), Brunei Darussalam (52.3%), and Algeria (48.5%) come close to parity. At the other extreme, Saudi Arabia (2.7%), Afghanistan (4.1%), and Andorra (0%) recorded the lowest shares.

Finding 04

Education & Health are Female-Majority by a Wide Margin

The feminisation of Education and Health & Welfare faculties is not marginal — it is structurally entrenched. Both fields exceed 70% female graduates on global average, and multiple countries report 95–100% female shares in these fields.

This has implications beyond gender statistics: if teaching and nursing pipelines are overwhelmingly female while engineering and STEM remain male-dominated, wage-gap and economic-participation gaps are, in part, an artefact of field-level sorting at tertiary level.

Finding 05

Data Coverage is Sparse — Results Should Be Read as Indicative

Of the 169,600 total rows in the raw dataset, only 11,901 (7.0%) contain actual observations. The average country has data for just 8.7 of 22 possible years, and coverage is heavily skewed toward Europe and Latin America, with large gaps in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia.

This matters for reconciliation: country rankings, top-10 lists, and trend lines in this analysis reflect where data exists, not a complete census. Countries with the highest reported values (e.g. 100% in Health for small island states) should be interpreted with caution — small absolute numbers of graduates can produce extreme percentages.

Summary

Five Things This Data Confirms

①
Field segregation is the dominant pattern
A 46-percentage-point gap between the most and least female-dominated fields dwarfs all country or time-period variation in the data.
②
Progress is real but slow
Every major field improved between 1998 and 2019. At current rates, Engineering will not reach parity for 70+ years.
③
Natural Sciences has crossed parity
From 49.2% to 57.3% — this field moved from male-majority to female-majority over the observation window. A meaningful milestone.
④
Some MENA and Latin American countries outperform OECD peers in Engineering
Algeria, Tunisia, and Argentina suggest that policy and cultural factors — not economic development level — are the key determinant of female engineering enrolment.
⑤
Data sparsity limits precision
With a 7% fill rate, findings are directionally robust but not statistically exhaustive. Policy conclusions should be supplemented with national-level administrative data.
